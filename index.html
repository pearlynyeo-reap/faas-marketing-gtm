Tier 1 — Why this is critical for FaaS success
Tier 1 is the GTM engine. Without it, Embedded Finance has no market presence, no pipeline, and no conversion path.
FaaS is being relaunched as a unified platform — but the market doesn't know that yet. Reap has the product, the proof points, and live clients, but none of that translates into revenue without a coordinated external motion. Tier 1 builds the four pillars that every deal depends on: the narrative that makes FaaS legible to buyers (Foundation), the content engine that creates organic demand at scale (Content & SEO), the outbound and inbound motion that turns interest into pipeline (Demand Gen), and the website that converts that pipeline into qualified conversations (Website). Regional Launches sits here too because HK, MX, and BR are already live — this isn't future planning, it's active execution in markets where Reap has real proof and real clients today. These five tracks run in parallel and are mutually dependent — a great website with no demand gen is invisible; demand gen without a website has nowhere to point. Getting Tier 1 right in Q3 is the difference between FaaS becoming the category leader in embedded finance or remaining an internal restructure that the market never notices.

Tier 2 — Why this is critical for FaaS success
Tier 2 is what keeps the GTM engine honest — converting in-person relationships into pipeline and ensuring every dollar and every effort is traceable back to outcomes.
In embedded finance, trust is built in rooms — not just on websites. Offline activations give FaaS the moments where proof points land in person, where a named client in the room does more than ten case studies online, and where the Payward partnership and Agentic narrative get their first public stage. Token2049 in particular is the single highest-leverage moment in H2 — it is Reap's first coordinated public signal that FaaS is real, live, and winning. Without measurement running in parallel, none of the Tier 1 investment can be optimised — Reap would be running six workstreams blind to each other, unable to tell which ICP converts best, which channel is generating real pipeline, or where deals are stalling in the sales cycle. Tier 2 is lighter touch on headcount but absolutely non-negotiable on outcomes.

Tier 3 Seeded now, deliberately slower-build — Q4 and beyond · Tracks 7, 8
Tier 3 — Why this is critical for FaaS success
Tier 3 is where Reap builds the moats that competitors cannot copy quickly — community trust among builders and the revenue flywheel inside the existing client base.
The fastest and cheapest revenue available to FaaS right now is not net-new acquisition — it is the existing CaaS client base that is already on the platform and doesn't know what else they can access. Track 8 is an extension of Customer Growth and should be treated with the same urgency as any net-new sales motion. Every client that expands to a second module is proof of platform stickiness, a better unit economics story, and a living case study. Track 7 is a longer play but a strategically important one — the embedded finance buyers of tomorrow are the Web3 developers and protocol builders active in communities today. Reap has no presence there yet. Building it now, even lightly, is the difference between being discovered organically in those spaces versus always having to buy attention. Both tracks compound over time in ways that paid acquisition cannot replicate.
